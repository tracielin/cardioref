?

Should my patient with coarctation of the aorta participate in organized sports?

  • Coarctation, unrepaired

    Need to know:

    • Ascending aorta dilation?
    • Exercise-induced hypertension?
    • Coarctation gradient

    ⬋             ⬊

    No ascending aorta dilation, normal exercise test, < 20mmHg gradient

    "Athletes with coarctation and without significant ascending aortic dilation (z score ≤3.0; a score of 3.0 equals 3 standard deviations from the mean for patient size) with a normal exercise test and a resting systolic blood pressure gradient < 20 mm Hg between the upper and lower limbs and a peak systolic blood pressure not exceeding the 95th percentile of predicted with exercise can participate in all competitive sports (Class I; Level of Evidence C)."

    All sports are likely acceptable, if the above conditions are met.

    Ascending aorta dilation, exercise-induced hypertension, or > 20mmHg gradient

    "Athletes with a systolic blood pressure arm/leg gradient >20 mm Hg or exercise-induced hypertension (a peak systolic blood pressure exceeding the 95th percentile of predicted with exercise) or with significant ascending aortic dilation (z score >3.0) may be considered for participation only in low-intensity class IA sports (Class IIb; Level of Evidence C)."

    May only consider low-intensity class IA sports (bowling, cricket, curling, golf, riflery, yoga)

  • Coarctation, treated by surgery or balloon and stent

    Need to know:

    • How long since repair
    • Residual gradient
    • Exercise test results
    • Ascending aorta dilation
    • Repair-site aneurysm
    • Aortic valve disease?

    ⬋             ⬊

    Greater than 3 months post op, < 20mmHg gradient, and all other conditions above normal

    "Athletes who are >3 months past surgical repair or stent placement with < 20 mm Hg arm/leg blood pressure gradient at rest, as well as (1) a normal exercise test with no significant dilation of the ascending aorta (z score < 3.0), (2) no aneurysm at the site of coarctation intervention, and (3) no significant concomitant aortic valve disease, may be considered for participation in competitive sports, but with the exception of high-intensity static exercise (classes IIIA, IIIB, and IIIC), as well as sports that pose a danger of bodily collision (Class IIb; Level of Evidence C)."

    May consider sports except high-intensity static exercise (IIIA, IIIB, IIIC) and those where danger of collision

    Aortic dilation or aneurysm

    "Athletes with evidence of significant aortic dilation or aneurysm formation (not yet at a size to need surgical repair) may be considered for participation only in low-intensity (classes IA and IB) sports (Class IIb; Level of Evidence C)."

    If not yet requiring additional surgical repair, may consider Class IA sports (Bowling, Cricket, Curling, Golf, Riflery, Yoga) or IB sports (Baseball/softball, Fencing, Table tennis, Volleyball)

Reference: Eligibility and Disqualification Recommendations for Competitive Athletes With Cardiovascular Abnormalities: Task Force 4: Congenital Heart Disease. A Scientific Statement From the AHA and ACC. Circulation 2015
